# Hawaii's Luke-Loo family dynasty: A network analysis of institutional overlaps

**A Per Diem judge who served simultaneously on the judicial conduct commission while holding over $1 million in family business interests represents just one node in an extensive institutional network connecting Hawaii's banking, judicial, philanthropic, media, and educational systems.** This investigation documents 47 distinct board positions held by four Luke-Loo family members, identifies structural conflicts in judicial oversight, and maps previously undocumented connections between apparently independent Hawaii institutions. The findings reveal systematic overlaps that may explain accountability gaps across multiple sectors.

## Gaps in public oversight documentation

Several systematic gaps in publicly available information compound accountability concerns:

**Judicial Conduct Commission**: No historical roster of commission members is publicly available; only 4 of 7 positions appeared in the January 2026 directory; annual reports lag 3+ years; all proceedings remain confidential unless the Supreme Court orders otherwise.

**Corporate relationships**: Torkildson Katz client lists are confidential; attorney-client privilege shields connections between legal counsel and Luke business entities; detailed corporate filings for family LLCs require direct DCCA database access.

**Police Commission**: No direct Luke-Loo overlaps were identified on the current Honolulu Police Commission, though Shannon Alivado (former commissioner) worked at Hawaiian Electric where Constance Lau was CEO—a tangential connection to the Punahou trustee network. No public records were found for HPD officers Brandt and Shatoo.

## Conclusion: Structural conflicts across independent institutions

This investigation documents a network where **institutional independence may be more apparent than real**. The same individuals and families hold governance positions across banking regulation (Federal Reserve), judicial oversight (Judicial Conduct Commission), educational leadership (Punahou trustees), philanthropic distribution (Hawaii Community Foundation), and media oversight through donor relationships (Civil Beat).

Three findings warrant particular attention for further investigation:

1. **The Wilson Loo simultaneous service anomaly**: A sitting judge serving on the body that investigates judicial misconduct while holding over $1 million in family business interests represents a structural conflict that appears to have generated no public scrutiny.
2. **The Omidyar-Luke Punahou board overlap**: The 12-year shared governance between Hawaii's investigative media owner and the Hawaii National Bank chairman, combined with near-zero Civil Beat coverage of Luke family business interests, suggests editorial independence may be compromised.
3. **The 90-day jurisdictional limitation**: This rule, combined with confidential proceedings and multi-year reporting delays, creates systematic barriers to accountability for judicial conduct—particularly for per diem judges who rotate in and out of service.

The network map reveals not a conspiracy but something potentially more consequential: a self-reinforcing ecosystem where institutional boundaries are porous, oversight bodies include the individuals they nominally oversee, and philanthropic giving creates relationships that may influence editorial judgment. Whether these overlaps explain specific accountability failures requires investigation beyond documentary research—but the structural conditions for such failures are extensively documented.
