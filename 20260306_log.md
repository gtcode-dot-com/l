I checked the official HPD/Honolulu sources for **anything** saying they can decline or reject a report. I found some **publicly stated authority to reject or refuse in narrow situations**, but I did **not** find a public HPD or Hawaii rule that lets a patrol officer refuse an **in-person Oʻahu complaint** just because the officer thinks, based on the “totality,” that the facts do not add up to harassment. ([Honolulu Police Department][1])

Here are the public exceptions I did find:

* **Online reports** can be screened. HPD’s web policy says an online report reviewer may ask follow-up questions if the report is incomplete, and may **reject** the report if it does not meet online-reporting criteria. HPD’s public police-report page says online reporting is not for emergencies, incidents needing an on-site officer, incidents outside Oʻahu, cases where the suspect is present or known, cases involving weapons, witnesses present, or motor-vehicle collisions. ([Honolulu Police Department][1])

* **Telephone/long-distance requests** can be declined in some cases. HPD’s Police Reports policy says complaints about criminal activity in **another jurisdiction** “shall not be accepted” without a formal request from another law-enforcement agency. It also says **noncriminal, nonemergency requests** like forwarding messages or running errands “shall not be accepted.” For emergency calls, the appropriate element may determine the **validity** of the call and take appropriate action. ([Honolulu Police Department][2])

* **Misconduct complaints against HPD employees** are routed and screened by category. HPD’s complaints policy routes on-duty officer misconduct within 60 days to the Honolulu Police Commission, later complaints to a supervisor/PSO, and individual/family matters that do not involve criminal law or standards-of-conduct issues to HRD. That is a kind of intake triage, but it is about **misconduct complaints against HPD personnel**, not about a patrol officer documenting your underlying incident with John Doe. ([Honolulu Police Department][3])

For **your fact pattern**, the public department-wide reporting rule still points the other way. HPD’s current Police Reports policy says an on-duty officer **must prepare a numbered report** each time the officer receives a complaint, and for **walk-in, on-beat, and mail complaints** the investigating officer must obtain the information needed to prepare the report and get a case number. The same policy separates **classification** from **intake**: Communications gives the initial classification, the investigating officer may later change it if warranted, and if the classification changes after the initial report, the officer documents that in a follow-up. The policy also says reports should be completed regardless of whether later prosecution or other action is likely. ([Honolulu Police Department][2])

So the only public “validity/screening” language I found is tied to **emergency calls**, **online-report eligibility**, **out-of-jurisdiction calls**, **nonpolice errands**, or **misconduct-complaint routing**. I did **not** find public authority saying a patrol officer on Oʻahu may refuse an **on-beat/walk-in crime complaint** because he personally decided the conduct was not harassment. ([Honolulu Police Department][2])

On your question about whether the public policy could be out of date or incomplete: **possibly incomplete in detail, yes; publicly superseded, I did not find that.** HPD’s current Directive System policy says department-wide policies apply across the department and remain in effect until rescinded by the Chief. It also says public copies may be redacted, and HPD’s website policy says web content must be accurate, is subject to annual assessment for discrepancies/updates, and must not conflict with departmental directives. HPD also has element-level Manuals of Operations with the force of directives, so there can be internal procedural detail that is not on the public website. But I did **not** find a public rescission or amendment creating a patrol-officer exception to skip the report in the situation you described. ([Honolulu Police Department][4])

My bottom line: **yes, HPD has public authority to reject some requests/reports in specific channels and circumstances; no, I did not find public authority for what happened to you.** The strongest public reading is still that the officer should have taken the complaint, assigned or obtained a report number, and then classified or reclassified it as appropriate. ([Honolulu Police Department][1])

The cleanest way to test whether there is a hidden exception is a records request for the **current operative version of Policy 8.06 (Police Reports)** plus any **District 1 / patrol / Alternative Call Servicing manual sections** on complaint intake, report rejection, screening, or reclassification.
