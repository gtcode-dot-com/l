The Wilson Loo Crimes: Judicial Misconduct in Plain Sight

The pattern of judicial corruption reached its apex in December 2022 with Judge Wilson M.N. Loo's alleged commission of multiple felonies during an injunction hearing. The case involved a federally-connected trust funder who had engaged in violence, stalking, and drug distribution against the victim.

Suborning Perjury: The Audio-Only Advantage

Judge Loo allegedly exploited his knowledge that the hearing was being recorded audio-only to engage in visual witness coaching. When the defendant was asked under oath about providing LSD to the plaintiff, Judge Loo allegedly made a clear non-verbal "no" gesture, inducing perjury. When the victim attempted to object and place this misconduct on the record, Loo allegedly cut him off aggressively, shouting "Nah ah ah enough out of you!!"

Legal Analysis: This constitutes suborning perjury under both Hawaii Revised Statutes § 710-1072.2 and federal law 18 U.S.C. § 1622. Judge Loo's previous service on the Commission on Judicial Conduct provided him intimate knowledge of oversight limitations, making this misconduct particularly calculated and egregious.

Phase VI: Institutional Cover-Up - The Corruption Loophole

The Strategic Resignation: Evading Accountability

When Judge Wilson Loo was reported for his courtroom crimes, the Hawaii Commission on Judicial Conduct facilitated what can only be described as a corruption loophole. According to their own correspondence, Loo was "no longer a per diem judge as of July 2024" - conveniently timing his departure to avoid their 90-day jurisdictional window.

However, Hawaii State Judiciary websites continued listing Wilson M.N. Loo as an active First Circuit Per Diem Judge, creating a contradiction. This suggests Loo may have strategically "resigned" only long enough to evade accountability before quietly returning to the bench.

The Audrey Stanley Connection: A Pattern of Misconduct in the Judiciary

The alleged corruption is not isolated to Wilson Loo. The history of another First Circuit Per Diem Judge, Audrey L.E. Stanley, reveals a disturbing pattern that further calls into question the integrity of Hawaii's judicial appointments.

During her tenure as a Public Defender, before her appointment to the bench, Ms. Stanley was allegedly informed of the conspired murder threat issued by Eugene and Rita Hartmann. Instead of fulfilling her duty to report a violent felony threat against a citizen, she allegedly participated in the cover-up. According to the victim's account, she relayed an extortionate offer: charges may be dropped if the victim agreed to leave the state.

The fact that an individual who was allegedly party to such a profound ethical breach could subsequently be vetted and appointed as a judge suggests a systemic failure in Hawaii's judicial selection process. This alleged past conduct, though only recently reported to the Commission, paints a picture of a system where officials can engage in serious misconduct and still ascend to the bench with impunity.
