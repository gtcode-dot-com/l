**I. Summary of Complaint**

This complaint concerns the conduct of an on-duty Honolulu Police Department patrol officer during a citizen contact at approximately 8:10 AM HST on March 6, 2026, in Waialua, Oʻahu. The complaint raises two issues:

1.  **Procedural violation:** The officer refused to prepare a numbered police report after receiving an in-person complaint, on the stated basis that the reported conduct did not constitute harassment. This is in apparent violation of HPD Policy 8.06 (Police Reports), which does not condition the duty to prepare a report on the officer's assessment of whether a crime has occurred. The policy states: *"An on-duty officer shall prepare a numbered report each time he or she receives a complaint."*

2.  **Unprofessional conduct:** When I attempted to explain the background of the situation, the officer yelled, "Stop telling me how to do my job!" When I requested the officer's badge number, the officer muttered it inaudibly, and when I stated loudly that I did not hear and asked the officer to repeat, the officer closed the vehicle window, activated emergency lights, and drove away. I captured the departure on video.

**II. Factual Narrative**

**A. The Incident Prompting the Call**

At approximately 8:10 AM HST on March 6, 2026, I was bicycling into the former Waialua Sugar Mill complex in Waialua. Jason Dean McKinley, operating a white Nissan Frontier pickup truck (approximately model year 2005), passed by me, made direct eye contact, and made a growling vocalization accompanied by an aggressive facial expression directed at me.

This contact was not isolated. McKinley is the subject of two prior HPD reports filed by me (report numbers 22-353421 and 22-365099), both documenting harassment and vehicle-related threat/intimidation incidents in the Haleʻiwa area. Those reports are part of a documented pattern of harassment by McKinley spanning from November 2021 to the present, including multiple vehicle-related intimidation incidents, a physical assault, and verbal harassment.

I called 911 at 8:12 AM HST and requested that a police officer respond so that I could file a police report.

**B. The Officer Contact**

A marked HPD patrol unit responded. The officer was operating a black Toyota 4Runner, marked, bearing Hawaiʻi license plate TXJ 254. Based on the vehicle and the officer's physical appearance, I believe this is the same Wahiawā beat officer who, on a prior occasion in approximately late July 2024, attempted to dissuade me from reporting a stolen wallet by threatening to confiscate my bicycle. On that occasion, the report was ultimately taken by the officer's partner.

I explained to the officer that I wished to file a police report regarding McKinley's conduct. The officer began explaining to me what constitutes harassment versus assault under the law, and told me that what I had described did not meet the definition of harassment. I attempted to explain the background and the broader documented pattern, but the officer did not allow me to get to that point. Before I had any opportunity to describe the prior HPD reports, the history of vehicle-based intimidation, or the other conduct underlying my complaint, the officer had already determined that no report would be taken.

The officer declined to take a report. The officer's stated basis was that the conduct I described did not constitute harassment—that is, the officer made a substantive determination that no crime had occurred and used that determination as justification for refusing to accept the complaint. The interaction did not progress far enough for me to articulate the full pattern or to discuss whether the conduct might also constitute stalking under HRS § 711-1106.5, because the officer had already decided no report was warranted. When I attempted to explain the history and basis for my complaint, the officer yelled at me: "Stop telling me how to do my job!"

I then requested the officer's badge number. The officer muttered a name and number at a volume I could not hear. I stated loudly that I did not hear and asked the officer to repeat the information. The officer closed the vehicle window, activated the vehicle's emergency lights, and drove away without providing the requested identification. I recorded the officer's departure, including the activation of emergency lights, on video. The use of emergency lights during departure from a citizen contact where the officer was being asked for identification raises the question of whether a call for service existed at that moment or whether the lights were activated as a pretext to leave. This should be verifiable against CAD/dispatch records for the relevant timestamp.

**C. Follow-Up Contact with PSO**

At approximately 9:10 AM HST on March 6, 2026, I contacted the Professional Standards Office and spoke with Detective Elliott, badge number 1196. Detective Elliott advised that complaints of misconduct within 60 days should be directed to the Honolulu Police Commission, and that complaints of misconduct and procedural violations could also be directed to PSO.

Detective Elliott stated that police officers can look at the "totality" of a situation and decline to file a report. This appears to restate the responding officer's position: that if a patrol officer personally concludes no crime has occurred, no report is required. However, HPD Policy 8.06 separates intake from classification. The policy obligates the officer to prepare a numbered report each time a complaint is received. Classification—determining what, if anything, the reported conduct constitutes---is a separate step that occurs after intake, and the policy expressly provides that classification may be changed later if warranted. The policy further states that reports should be completed regardless of whether later prosecution or other action is likely. In other words, the officer's role at the point of contact is to document the complaint, not to adjudicate it. I was unable to locate any publicly available HPD directive, policy, or amendment that authorizes an on-duty patrol officer to refuse an in-person, on-beat complaint because the officer has concluded the reported conduct does not amount to a crime.

I note for the record that Detective Elliott articulated the responding officer's legal justification to me during the PSO intake call—before I had filed this complaint and before any investigation had been initiated. Specifically, Detective Elliott volunteered the "totality" standard as the basis for an officer's authority to decline a report, which is the same substantive position the responding officer took during the contact. I am not alleging that Detective Elliott acted improperly in describing how the department views officer discretion. I am documenting that the reviewing body's stated framework for evaluating this complaint was communicated to me as the responding officer's defense before the complaint was submitted. If the disposition of this complaint ultimately rests on the same "totality" standard that Detective Elliott described—a standard I have been unable to locate in any published HPD directive---I ask that the basis for that standard be identified in writing in any disposition letter, so that the reasoning is reviewable.

**III. Applicable Policy**

**HPD Policy 8.06 (Police Reports)** provides, in relevant part:

> *"An on-duty officer shall prepare a numbered report each time he or she receives a complaint."*

The same policy provides that for walk-in, on-beat, and mail complaints, the investigating officer must obtain the information needed to prepare the report and obtain a case number. The policy separates classification from intake: Communications assigns the initial classification, and the investigating officer may later change it if warranted. The policy further provides that reports should be completed regardless of whether later prosecution or other action is likely.

I have reviewed HPD's publicly available directives, including Policy 8.06 (Police Reports), the online reporting policy, and the complaints and internal investigations policy. The public exceptions I identified are limited to:

-   Online reports that do not meet online-reporting criteria;

-   Telephone complaints about criminal activity in another jurisdiction, absent a formal request from another law-enforcement agency;

-   Noncriminal, nonemergency requests such as forwarding messages or running errands; and

-   Emergency calls where the appropriate element may determine the validity of the call.

None of these exceptions applies to this contact. I was physically present in the officer's patrol area. The complaint concerned conduct occurring on Oʻahu. The subject was identified by name and had two prior HPD report numbers on file. The officer's stated justification---that the conduct did not constitute harassment---is not among the published exceptions. Even if the officer believed the single morning contact, viewed in isolation, did not meet the elements of harassment, that belief does not excuse the refusal to document the complaint. Policy 8.06 does not condition the duty to prepare a report on the officer's agreement that a crime has been committed. It conditions the duty on the receipt of a complaint. I made a complaint. The officer received it. The report should have been prepared.
